\begin{Exercise}[title={A Nice Bound for Log}, difficulty=2, label=7a1]
Show that for any $0<x<y$, we have the inequality $\dfrac{1}{y} < \dfrac{\ln(y)-\ln(x)}{y-x}< \dfrac{1}{x}$. \hfill \emph{solution} \refAnswer{7a1}

\end{Exercise}

\begin{Answer}[ref={7a1}]
	Note that the term bounded by $\dfrac{1}{y}$ and $\dfrac{!}{x}$ is the average rate of change of $\ln(x)$. So applying the MVT gives that there is some $c\in [x,y]$ such that $\dfrac{d}{dx} \ln(x)$ at $x=c$ is equal to the average rate of change of log. Finally use that $y=\dfrac{1}{x}$ is decreasing.
	
\end{Answer}
