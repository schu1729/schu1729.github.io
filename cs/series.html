Series Notation

Series involve summing the terms of a sequence. So first let us talk about the way we will notate this summation.

Partial Sum

The partial sum $s_m$ of a sequence $a_k$ is the sum of the first $m$ terms of the sequence. ie. $s_m=a_1+a_2+\ldots +a_m$.

We won't always want to sum from the first term to the $m$th term so we also have sigma notation. Again let us not specify the sequence but notate it as $a_k$ and suppose we want to express the partial sum $s_m$ in sigma notation. It would look like: $\displaystyle \sum \limits_{k=1}^m a_k$. Let's see this in a few examples. Don't worry about evaluating any of the sums yet.
In each of these examples think about how sigma notation is constructed and why it is generally the preferred notation for summation.
  • Let $a_n=\{1,1,1,1,1,\ldots\}$, or $a_n=1$. Then summing the first 15 terms would be $1+1+1+\ldots+1$ fifteen times or 15(1). In sigma notation: $\displaystyle \sum \limits_{k=1}^{15} 1 =15$.
  • Let $b_k=\{1,-2,4,-8,16,\ldots\}$ or $b_k=(-2)^{k-1}$. Summing the 3rd term through the 12th term requires the use of sigman notation: $\displaystyle \sum \limits_{k=1}^{12} (-2)^{k-1}$.
  • We will see later that we can occasionally sum an infinite number of terms and get a finite result. Let $g_n=\frac{1}{3}^{n-1}$ then the sum of the first term and all following terms can be expressed in partial sums as $s_\infty$, but sigma notation is more commonly used in this situation: $\displaystyle \sum \limits_{k=1}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^{k-1}$
